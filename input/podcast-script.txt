Here's a question that keeps engineering managers up at night. How do you know if your team is doing well? And the scarier version: how do you know if one particular person is doing well? Software is one of the hardest things in the world to measure. The work is invisible, the quality only shows up months later, and the most valuable thing someone did all week might have been preventing a disaster that now leaves no trace. So today, let's talk about how to measure software work without fooling yourself, and without wrecking the very thing you're trying to improve.

Let's start with the single most important idea, because if you only remember one thing, make it this one. It's called Goodhart's Law, and it goes like this. When a measure becomes a target, it stops being a good measure. Let that sink in, because it's sneaky.

Here's what it means in practice. Say you decide to measure how many pull requests each developer merges. A pull request, by the way, is just a bundle of code changes submitted for review before it joins the main project. Think of it like handing in a chunk of homework. Now, the moment people know you're counting those bundles, they start making them smaller and more frequent, just to push the number up. The count goes up. The actual work doesn't. You measured the wrong thing and you got exactly what you asked for.

This happens with everything. Count lines of code, and people write more verbose code. Count story points, those rough effort estimates teams use, and the estimates quietly inflate. Count bugs, and suddenly bugs get relabeled as "improvements." Every one of these has been tried, and every one has been gamed, usually without anyone even meaning to.

So does that mean metrics are useless? Not at all. Here's the reframe that makes everything click. Metrics are thermometers, not steering wheels. A thermometer tells you the room is cold. It tells you where to look and what to ask about. But you'd never try to steer a car with a thermometer. In the same way, a metric should point you toward a good question, and it should never, ever directly decide someone's bonus, their ranking, or their punishment. The instant a number controls someone's paycheck, it stops describing reality and starts describing what people think you want to see.

Okay. So now that we know metrics are for asking questions, not issuing verdicts, let's talk about what's actually worth measuring. And the big insight here is about where you point the lens. The best team-level metrics measure the system, not the individuals inside it.

The gold standard here is a set of four metrics known as DORA. That stands for DevOps Research and Assessment, and it came out of years of research into what actually makes software teams effective. What's clever about DORA is that it's really two pairs. Two of the metrics measure speed, and two measure stability. On the speed side, you look at how often you ship new code to your users, and how long it takes for a change to go from a developer's laptop to actually running live. On the stability side, you look at how often your releases break something, and how quickly you recover when they do.

And here's the beautiful part, the reason it works. The two pairs keep each other honest. Imagine a team that wants to look fast, so they start shipping recklessly, over and over. Their speed numbers shoot up. But all that reckless shipping breaks things, so their failure rate climbs right alongside it, and the mask slips. You simply cannot look good on all four at once without genuinely being good. The pairing is the whole trick. Speed alone is easy to fake. Speed plus stability is not.

So that's the system. Now let's get concrete about one specific kind of system health that everyone complains about but nobody can pin down. Technical debt.

Technical debt is a lovely metaphor. When you take a shortcut in your code to ship faster today, it's like borrowing money. It works right now, but you'll pay interest on it later, in the form of every future change being a little slower and a little more painful. The problem is, you can't put a single honest number on technical debt. So the trick is not to find the one perfect number. The trick is to triangulate, to look at several imperfect signals from different angles and see where they agree.

Let me give you the two most useful ones. The first is finding your hotspots. You cross-reference two things: which files change the most often, and which files are the most complicated. A file that's a tangled mess but nobody ever touches? Leave it alone, it's harmless. But a file that's both a tangled mess and gets changed every single week? That, right there, is where your bugs breed and your developers suffer. That's the debt worth paying down first.

The second signal is the one that convinces the people holding the budget. It's the interest you're already paying. Look at how much of your team's time goes to unplanned work, the emergency bug fixes, the "why is this taking three days" surprises. If forty percent of every two-week sprint is getting eaten by unplanned firefighting, that's not vague grumbling anymore. That's your interest rate, in plain numbers, and it's an argument any manager understands.

Now for the most dangerous territory of all. Measuring individual people. And here the research and the scar tissue agree on something blunt. Ranking individuals against each other by their metrics reliably destroys teams. Why? Because it rewards loud, visible, solo output, and it quietly punishes all the things that actually make a team great. Reviewing a teammate's code carefully. Mentoring a junior. Writing the documentation. Taking on the ugly cleanup job nobody else wants. None of that shows up as a personal high score, so a ranking system treats it as invisible, and your best people learn to stop doing it.

So what can you do instead? Two things really matter. First, measure impact, not output. The question is never "how many code bundles did this person ship?" It's "what actually changed because this person was here?" Did the checkout page start failing less often? Did reviews across the whole team get faster after they fixed a broken process? That's a story you build together with the person, not a number you scrape off a dashboard.

Second, watch for the multipliers, and watch for the glue. Your strongest engineers often show up in other people's numbers. The code they reviewed ships with fewer defects. The juniors they mentored get up to speed faster. And someone on your team is quietly keeping the whole machine running, updating dependencies, answering questions, keeping the automated tests green. That work is essential and completely invisible to any tool. If you don't go looking for it on purpose, you will undervalue those people, and one day they'll leave, and only then will you feel how much they were holding up.

So let's bring it all home. Metrics are thermometers, not steering wheels. They start conversations, they don't hand down verdicts. Measure your system relentlessly, because the system is fair game and hard to fake. Measure your team carefully, using balanced pairs like speed against stability so no single number can be gamed. And measure individuals almost not at all. Look at their real impact and the way they lift the people around them, and beyond that, just talk to them.

Because here's the real goal, the thing underneath all of this. The point of measurement in software was never precision. It's earlier, better conversations. A good setup doesn't tell you who to promote or fire. It tells you where friction is building up and which questions to ask before a small problem becomes a crisis. So this week, try one thing. Look at a number your team already tracks, and instead of judging someone with it, turn it into a curious question. That one shift changes everything.
